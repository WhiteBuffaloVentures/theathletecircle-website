## The Five Roles in Your Circle

**Q: What are the five roles in The Circle?**
A: Each athlete needs five defined advisory roles. Each role has a clear function. Each role stays in its lane.

1. **Lawyer** — Contract review, structural protection, compliance. Catches the dangerous clauses. Never also handle your money or business advice.

2. **Agent** — Sourcing opportunities, negotiation, market positioning. Your market arm. Gets you in the room. Never also handle your legal matters or money.

3. **Financial Advisor** — Budgeting, tax structure, investment, long-term wealth. Builds your financial system. Never sells you investment products dressed as advice.

4. **Trainer** — Performance, conditioning, recovery. Your performance partner. Never becomes an unofficial manager or agent.

5. **Mentor** — Perspective, character, honest counsel. Knows you as a person. Not a paid guru. Not a life coach. A trusted advisor who can say hard things.

**Q: Do I need all five people right now?**
A: Not necessarily. You might start with three or four. But the framework tells you what's missing. Each gap is a vulnerability.

**Q: What happens if one person fills two roles?**
A: That's the most dangerous pattern we see. The agent who "also handles money." The family member who "also handles legal." It feels efficient. It compounds risk. Each combination creates a conflict of interest that bends decisions away from your long-term outcome.

**Q: How do I know if my Circle is working?**
A: Run the Circle Audit. For each role, ask:
1. Is this role filled? (If not — that's a gap)
2. Is the person also filling another role? (If yes — that's a conflict)
3. Are their incentives aligned with yours? (If you're not sure — have the conversation)

## The Five Decisions

### DECISION 1: WHO GETS CLOSE?

**Q: Why does it matter who's in my circle?**
A: The single most important predictor of how your career and finances unfold is not talent. It's the quality of the people standing closest to your decisions. Talent opens the door. The system is what walks through it.

**Q: What happens if my circle isn't right?**
A: You make decisions under pressure that you'd never make in a calm room. An agent chases volume instead of protecting you. A family member gives business advice they're not qualified to give. A financial advisor sells you investments instead of building your wealth. All of this costs you money, opportunities, and years.

---

### DECISION 2: WHO REPRESENTS YOU?

**Q: What does an agent really do?**
A: An agent finds opportunities, negotiates terms, and positions you to win in the market. But most athletes choose agents the way they choose restaurants — whoever has the best pitch and nicest office. The stakes are too high for that.

**Q: How much commission does an agent take?**
A: Commission comes out of YOUR earnings. A 15% deal on a $20K NIL contract means $3K goes to the agent and $17K (gross, before taxes) goes to you. Get the full commission structure in writing — across NIL, endorsements, and playing contracts.

**Q: What should I ask a potential agent?**
A: Four questions matter most:
1. **What's your full commission structure** — across NIL, endorsement, and playing contracts? Get specifics in writing.
2. **Walk me through the last three deals you closed** for athletes at my level. What did you negotiate UP from the brand's first offer?
3. **What happens if I want to leave in six months?** Agents who flinch at this question have contracts designed to trap you.
4. **Tell me about a deal you turned down** for an athlete because it wasn't right. Agents who never say no aren't protecting you. They're chasing volume.

**Q: How do I know if it's time to fire my agent?**
A: If they're playing two roles (also handling your money or legal), if they pressure you into deals, if they're not transparent about commission, or if they're not chasing real value — it's time. Your default response to pressure: "My team handles all commercial conversations. Send the proposed terms in writing."

### DECISION 3: WHICH DEALS TO TAKE?

**Q: How do I evaluate a NIL deal?**
A: Every offer needs to pass through a five-question filter — before you respond. You stop at the first one that fails.

**Filter 1 — Values Alignment:** Does this brand align with my values and long-term identity? Would you be comfortable with this partnership being the lead line of your profile in five years? In ten? If the answer is anything other than a clear yes, you're done.

**Filter 2 — What am I actually committing to?** Read the deliverables out loud. Read the exclusivity terms out loud. The volume of work hidden behind "one Instagram post" is the most common reason athletes feel cheated.

**Filter 3 — Is the price right?** There's a working market rate based on your audience, engagement, sport, and visibility. If a brand offers a fraction of the going rate, they're banking on you not knowing the market.

**Filter 4 — What does this deal lock me out of?** Every deal you sign closes other doors. A $5K energy drink deal with category exclusivity can block a $200K sports drink opportunity three months later.

**Filter 5 — Am I structurally ready?** Compliance notified? Entity set up? Tax reserve funded? Lawyer reviewed? Even a perfect deal can hurt you if you don't have the infrastructure.

**Q: What's a realistic market rate?**
A: It depends on your audience tier:
- **Under 10K followers:** $200–$1K per post | $1K–$5K for 4-week package
- **10K–50K followers:** $1K–$5K per post | $5K–$20K for 4-week package
- **50K–250K followers:** $5K–$15K per post | $20K–$75K for 4-week package
- **250K+ followers:** $15K+ per post | $75K+ for 4-week package

Sport, position visibility, engagement rate, and brand budget all shift the range.

**Q: What should I never agree to verbally?**
A: Four things — even said casually on a phone call, they can be argued as binding:
1. "I'll wear it on game day"
2. "I'll post X times"
3. "That sounds good — let's do it"
4. "I won't sign with anyone else in the meantime"

Your default response: "My team handles all commercial conversations. Can you send the proposed terms in writing?"

### DECISION 4: WHAT YOU SIGN?

**Q: What are the most dangerous clauses in athlete contracts?**
A: Five red flags show up repeatedly:

1. **Perpetual Rights** — The brand can use your name, image, and content forever, long after the deal ends. Watch for "in perpetuity," "indefinite," "forever." Fix: Limit to 12–24 month license with optional renewal.

2. **Unlimited Exclusivity** — You can't sign with other brands in a broadly defined category. Watch for "exclusive worldwide," "any related category." Fix: Define narrowly. List specific competitors. Time-limit it.

3. **Morality Clauses** — The brand can terminate and claw back payments at its sole discretion. Watch for "objectionable conduct," "sole discretion." Fix: Require objective standards (actual conviction, documented breach).

4. **Vague Deliverables** — The work is left open to interpretation. Brand expects more than you planned. Watch for "reasonable amount," "as needed," "mutually agreed." Fix: Itemize everything — number of posts, platforms, dates, approval flow.

5. **No Exit Clause** — Multi-year term with no way out. Watch for silence on termination rights. Fix: Cap at 1 year. Add 30-day no-cause termination.

**Q: How should I handle verbal pressure from a brand or agent?**
A: Never agree verbally. Every time, with everyone, no exceptions: "My team handles all commercial conversations. Can you send the proposed terms in writing? I'll get on a call with my agent and lawyer to walk through it."

**Q: Why does my lawyer need to review everything?**
A: Because brands bury risk in contract language. Your lawyer catches patterns — perpetual rights, unlimited exclusivity, clawback clauses, non-compete language — that you might miss. The deal that seems fair at first glance can lock you out of opportunities worth 10x more.

### DECISION 5: WHO YOU'RE BECOMING?

**Q: What is the Athlete Operating System?**
A: The first four decisions are reactive — they fire when something happens to you. This one is proactive. It's a system that makes most of your small decisions in advance, so you don't make them all under pressure.

**Q: What are the five pillars?**
A: 
1. **Eligibility** — Know your school's NIL policy. Save your compliance officer's contact. Disclose proactively. Never sign anything without legal review.

2. **Circle** — Five roles. Quarterly review. No role doubles up. Monthly mentor connection. Annual legal review of all active agreements.

3. **Brand** — Write your brand promise in three sentences. Read it before every content decision. Post intentionally, not reactively. Update your highlight reel quarterly.

4. **Finance** — 30% of every NIL dollar goes to tax reserve. 10% goes to long-term investing. Quarterly CPA check-in. No major purchases without 72 hours and Circle review.

5. **Legacy** — Once a quarter, write one page on what you want to be known for in 10 years. Run every major decision through that page. Family conversations about money happen openly.

**Q: What's the Quarterly Review?**
A: Once a quarter, sit down (alone or with your closest advisor) and answer four questions:
1. What worked last quarter? (Deals signed, deals declined, relationships that produced, content that moved the needle)
2. Where's the weakest pillar? (Score each 1–5. The lowest score is the work)
3. What are the 1–3 most important things in the next 90 days?
4. Is what I'm doing aligned with who I'm becoming? (Read your legacy page. If the answer is no, something needs to change)

## The Connor Barry Story

**Q: Who is Connor Barry?**
A: Connor Barry is the athlete The Athlete Circle framework was built alongside. His story isn't extraordinary — it's a blueprint.

Connor was named Northern Virginia Football Hall of Fame Player of the Year after leading James Madison High School to a state runner-up finish. Despite those numbers, he received zero FBS scholarship offers. He walked on at Appalachian State as a freshman, witnessed an amazing season, but saw limited playing time when his offensive coordinator and QB coach were recruited to another school.

Most athletes' stories end there. Connor's didn't. He transferred to Christopher Newport University, a Division III program, and rebuilt his career from the ground up. Over three seasons, he became CNU's all-time leader in touchdown passes (55), passing efficiency (185.2), and yards per pass attempt (10.5). As a junior, he led CNU to its first-ever undefeated regular season (10-0), was named NJAC Offensive Player of the Year, earned AFCA Second-Team All-American honors, and became the first player in CNU's 25-year history to be named a Gagliardi Trophy semifinalist.

In January 2026, Penn State head coach Matt Campbell offered him a scholarship. Connor committed the same day. Within weeks, he was taking first-team reps in spring practice.

**Q: What made Connor's path work when other athletes didn't?**
A: He built his Circle before the spotlight arrived. His agent handles all commercial conversations — no exceptions. His family runs quarterly reviews. His brand is anchored in faith and family. Every NIL opportunity is filtered through that identity before price is ever discussed. He turned down deals that paid well but didn't align. He avoided verbal commitments. He operated the same boring system, week after week, while building something designed to last decades — not months.

Connor isn't a five-star recruit. He's not a Heisman frontrunner. He's a Big Ten quarterback with a clear identity, a structured Circle, and a system. That's the model.
